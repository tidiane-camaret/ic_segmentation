\begin{abstract}
In-context learning (ICL) enables medical image segmentation models to adapt to new anatomical structures from limited examples, reducing the clinical annotation burden. However, standard ICL methods typically rely on dense, global cross-attention, which scales poorly with image resolution. While recent approaches have introduced localized attention mechanisms, they often lack explicit supervision on the selection process, leading to redundant computation in non-informative regions. We propose a hierarchical framework that combines explicit patch selection with multi-level supervision. By supervising the model at both the patch and global levels, our approach learns to actively identify and attend only to the most informative anatomical regions. Evaluated on TotalSegmentator, our method achieves competitive in-domain CT segmentation accuracy while significantly reducing computational overhead for high-resolution volumes compared to full-context baselines. Training and evaluation code are available at \url{https://anonymous.4open.science/r/ic_segmentation}.

\section{Related Work}

\textbf{Global In-Context Learning.}

Early adaptations of ICL for segmentation, such as UniverSeg \cite{butoiUniverSegUniversalMedical2023}, utilize encoder-decoder architectures with dense cross-attention (CrossBlock) to fuse target and context features. Similarly, Iris \cite{gaoShowSegmentUniversal2025} encodes context pairs into global task embeddings.

\textit{Limitation:} These methods require processing the full resolution of both target and context images to compute attention maps, resulting in quadratic complexity $\mathcal{O}((HW)^2)$ that prohibits scaling to large inputs.

\textbf{Selective Attention and Retrieval.}

To address scalability, recent works have moved toward selective processing. Tyche \cite{rakicTycheStochasticInContext2025} introduces stochasticity to explore multiple potential masks, effectively attending to different "modes" of the output distribution. Other approaches, such as EICSeg \cite{xieEICSegUniversalMedical2026}, employ retrieval mechanisms to align features before processing.

\textit{Limitation:} While these methods focus attention on specific zones or features, this selection is often implicit : learned solely through the final segmentation loss without direct supervision on what to select. Consequently, they often still require encoding the entire image volume before the attention mechanism can discard irrelevant regions.

\textbf{Patch-based and Efficient Approaches.}

Direct patch-based methods like Medverse \cite{huMedverseUniversalModel2025a} use sliding window inference to handle high resolutions, while Patchwork \cite{reisertDeepNeuralPatchworks2022a} employs a hierarchical stacking strategy to iteratively sample uncertain regions.

\textit{Limitation:} Pure sliding windows treat patches independently, severing global context.


\textbf{Our Approach:} Unlike implicit attention methods, we employ explicit patch selection trained via multi-level supervision. This allows our model to discard uninformative background regions before heavy processing, retaining global context efficiency without the computational cost of full-volume attention.

\section{Method}

\noindent \textbf{Input:} Target image $I^t \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times W}$ and context set $\{(I^c_i, L^c_i)\}_{i=1}^{N_c}$ of image/label mask pairs.

\noindent \textbf{Output:} Predicted segmentation mask $\hat{L}^t$ at original resolution.

\subsection{Overview}

Our method processes images through a coarse-to-fine cascade of $M$ resolution levels $\{r_1, \ldots, r_M\}$ (e.g., $24 \rightarrow 96 \rightarrow 128$). At each level:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Sampling:} Select $K$ patches via Gumbel-top-$K$ sampling, using uncertainty from the previous level as sampling weights.
    \item \textbf{Attention:} Process target and context patches jointly through transformer layers.
    \item \textbf{Aggregation:} Combine potentially overlapping patch predictions of that level via averaging.
    \item \textbf{Refinement:} Additively fuse with upsampled previous-level predictions.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Differentiable Patch Sampling}

We use Gumbel-top-$K$ sampling to select patches in a differentiable manner:
\begin{equation}
    \text{idx} = \text{top-}K\left(\frac{w(x,y)}{\tau} + g_{x,y}\right), \quad g_{x,y} = -\log(-\log(u)), \; u \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)
\end{equation}
where $w(x,y)$ are sampling weights and $\tau$ is a temperature parameter.

At level $\ell > 1$, target patch sampling is guided by the entropy of the previous level's prediction:
\begin{equation}
    w_\ell(x, y) = H(p_{\ell-1}(x,y)), \quad H(p) = -p\log p - (1-p)\log(1-p)
\end{equation}
where $p_{\ell-1} = \sigma(\text{logits}_{\ell-1})$, focusing computation on uncertain regions. Context patches are sampled using the entropy of the ground-truth mask, balancing foreground coverage with boundary regions.

Patches are sampled such that their \textbf{center} lies within the image, allowing partial overlap with boundaries. This increases coverage of border regions often undersampled by interior-only methods.

\subsection{Feature Extraction and Encoding}

Patches are encoded using the frozen encoder from UniverSeg~\cite{butoiUniverSegUniversalMedical2023}. A lightweight CNN encoder projects these features to embeddings $\mathbf{z}_k \in \mathbb{R}^{d}$, producing skip connections for the decoder.

To enable a single backbone across all resolution levels, we condition on resolution via sinusoidal encoding, allowing generalization to unseen resolutions.

\subsection{Cross-Patch Attention}

Target and context patch embeddings are processed jointly through $L$ transformer layers with:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{2D RoPE:} Rotary position embeddings encode spatial patch coordinates $(y, x)$, enabling reasoning about relative patch positions.
    \item \textbf{Type embeddings:} Per-layer learnable embeddings distinguish target from context patches.
    \item \textbf{Bidirectional attention:} All patches attend to all others (Flash Attention compatible).
\end{itemize}

A U-Net style CNN decoder reconstructs patch-level predictions using skip connections from the encoder.

\subsection{Aggregation and Level Combination}

Patch predictions are aggregated using simple averaging over overlapping regions:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{L}^t_\ell(x,y) = \frac{\sum_{k} \mathbf{1}_{(x,y) \in P_k} \cdot p_k(x,y)}{\sum_{k} \mathbf{1}_{(x,y) \in P_k}}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{1}_{(x,y) \in P_k}$ indicates pixel $(x,y)$ is covered by patch $k$.

Level predictions are combined via additive fusion within the coverage mask:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{L}^t_{\text{comb}} = \hat{L}^t_{\ell-1,\uparrow} + M_\ell \odot \hat{L}^t_\ell
\end{equation}
where $\hat{L}^t_{\ell-1,\uparrow}$ is the upsampled previous prediction and $M_\ell$ is the binary coverage mask indicating which pixels were sampled at level $\ell$.

\subsection{Training}

The model is trained with supervision at each level using uniform weights:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L} = \sum_{\ell=1}^{M} \mathcal{L}_\text{combined}^\ell, \quad \mathcal{L}_\text{combined}^\ell = \mathcal{L}_\text{softdice}^\ell + \mathcal{L}_\text{focal}^\ell
\end{equation}
using the sum of soft Dice and Focal loss. During training, oracle sampling combines ground-truth foreground with entropy to balance coverage and uncertainty. \textbf{Oracle scheduling} linearly decays the oracle probability from 50\% to 0\% over the first epochs, transitioning to purely model-based (entropy-guided) sampling.

\section{Experiments}

\subsection{Dataset and Preprocessing}

We train and evaluate on TotalSegmentator~\cite{wasserthalTotalSegmentatorRobustSegmentation2023}, a large-scale CT dataset with 104 anatomical structures. To test true in-context generalization, we split by \textbf{label} rather than by case: 50 structures for training, 50 held-out structures for validation (Table~\ref{tab:label_splits}). This ensures the model must generalize to entirely unseen anatomical categories at test time, not just unseen patients.

\textbf{2D Slice Extraction.} We extract 2D slices from 3D volumes along all three anatomical axes (axial, coronal, sagittal), increasing data diversity and providing multi-view perspectives of each structure. Slices are filtered by coverage: only slices with $\geq 30$ foreground pixels are retained, preventing training on uninformative near-empty slices.

\textbf{Class-Balanced Sampling.} To prevent large organs (e.g., liver, lungs) from dominating training, we sample labels uniformly at each step. Within each label, we subsample to at most 6 slices per (case, axis) group using strided selection for even spatial distribution.

\textbf{Context Selection.} Context examples are preferentially drawn from the \textbf{same patient} as the target, providing anatomically consistent support. When insufficient same-case examples exist, we fall back to other patients with the same structure. We use $N_c = 5$ context pairs during training.

\textbf{Evaluation.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{In-domain:} TotalSegmentator CT (validation labels, all cases)
    \item \textbf{Out-of-domain:} TotalSegmentator MRI~\cite{dantonoliTotalSegmentatorMRIRobust2025} (all labels, all cases)
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Generalization to unseen dimensions}
\subsubsection{Input image size}

We evaluate how both methods scale with input resolution on TotalSegmentator CT validation set (Figure~\ref{fig:dice_flops_per_input_size}). PatchICL achieves higher Dice scores at all resolutions while requiring significantly less compute at high resolutions: at 512$\times$512, PatchICL uses 308 GFLOPs compared to UniverSeg's 549 GFLOPs (44\% reduction) while maintaining a +1.7\% accuracy advantage. Both methods peak at 256$\times$256 resolution, suggesting diminishing returns from higher resolutions on this benchmark. The favorable scaling of PatchICL stems from its patch-based attention, which processes only selected regions rather than the full image.
